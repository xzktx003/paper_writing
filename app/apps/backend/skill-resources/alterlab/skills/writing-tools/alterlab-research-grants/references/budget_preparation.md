# Budget Preparation and Justification

> ⚠️ **Verify current figures**: Salary caps, indirect-cost rates, modular thresholds, and equipment definitions change. Always confirm the numbers against your sponsored-programs office and the current agency policy before submission. The values below are illustrative of the categories and logic, not authoritative current rates.

## Overview

A budget is a financial argument for your research plan. Reviewers and program officers read it for **realism** (does the cost match the work?) and **alignment** (does every dollar map to an activity in the narrative?). An inflated, padded, or internally inconsistent budget undermines an otherwise strong proposal.

**Core Principle**: Build the budget *from* the research plan, not the other way around. Every line item should trace to a specific aim, task, or deliverable.

## Standard Budget Categories

- **Personnel (Salaries and Wages)**: PI, co-investigators, postdocs, graduate students, technicians, and staff. The largest line in most research budgets.
- **Fringe Benefits**: Calculated as a percentage of salary using your institution's negotiated fringe rate.
- **Equipment**: Items above the agency/institution capitalization threshold (commonly $5,000, but confirm) and with a useful life beyond one year. Equipment is typically excluded from the indirect-cost base.
- **Travel**: Conferences, collaboration visits, fieldwork, and required program/PI meetings (especially for DARPA and DOE).
- **Materials and Supplies**: Consumables, reagents, small software licenses, and lab supplies below the equipment threshold.
- **Other Direct Costs**: Publication/page charges, participant incentives, consultant fees, computing/cloud costs, and service-center charges.
- **Subawards / Consortium Costs**: Costs for collaborating institutions, each with their own budget and justification.
- **Indirect Costs (F&A / Overhead)**: Institutional facilities and administrative costs, applied to the modified total direct cost base at your negotiated rate.

## Personnel Effort

Effort is the heart of most budgets and the most scrutinized line.

- Express effort in **person-months** (calendar, academic, or summer) consistent with the agency convention.
- Effort must be **commensurate with the work**: a PI claiming negligible effort on a large, complex project raises feasibility concerns; over-committing across multiple grants triggers compliance flags.
- Track total committed effort across all active and pending awards to avoid exceeding 12 person-months (100%).
- Justify *why* each person is needed and *what* they will do, not merely their title.

## Agency-Specific Considerations

### NSF
- Full budget justification required for all line items.
- Salary support for senior personnel is conventionally limited (historically up to two months of regular salary per year across all NSF awards — confirm current policy).
- Cost sharing is generally prohibited unless explicitly required by the solicitation.
- Graduate student and postdoc support is encouraged and viewed favorably.

### NIH
- **Modular budgets** are available for applications at or below a direct-cost threshold per year (historically $250,000 in $25,000 modules) for many R-series mechanisms; larger or more complex awards use detailed budgets.
- A **salary cap** limits the salary base on which support can be requested; confirm the current cap each fiscal year.
- Justify personnel effort and key resources clearly even in modular budgets (the personnel justification is still required).

### DOE
- Cost sharing is often required, especially for applied programs and ARPA-E.
- Detailed budgets, frequently with annual or quarterly breakdowns, are expected.
- National-laboratory collaborations are typically budgeted separately and may follow different rules.
- Institutional commitment letters are commonly required for cost share.

### DARPA
- Budgets are structured by **phase and task**, mirroring the technical milestones.
- Large procurements require supporting cost data.
- Contract structures (e.g., cost-plus, firm-fixed-price) vary by solicitation and shape the budget format.
- Include travel for required program/PI meetings and demonstrations.

### NSTC (Taiwan)
- Budget categories and ceilings are defined by the program solicitation; confirm per-call limits.
- Justify personnel (including research assistants), equipment, and operational/business-travel costs against the project plan.

## Writing the Budget Justification

The justification narrates the budget. For each category:

- **Personnel**: Name (or role), effort in person-months, and a one-to-two sentence statement of responsibilities tied to specific aims.
- **Equipment**: Identify each item, its cost basis, and why it is essential and not already available.
- **Travel**: Specify destinations/purpose (e.g., "one domestic conference per year to disseminate results"; "annual PI meeting required by the solicitation").
- **Materials and Supplies**: Group logically and tie to experimental volume (e.g., per-sample reagent costs × planned samples).
- **Other Direct Costs**: Justify consultants by role and rate, computing by workload, and participant costs by study design.
- **Subawards**: Summarize each collaborator's scope and reference their attached budget.

## Multi-Year Budgets and Escalation

- Apply realistic salary escalation consistent with institutional policy (commonly a few percent per year — confirm the allowed rate).
- Align spending with the timeline: front-load setup and equipment, ramp personnel as the project scales, and reserve later years for analysis and dissemination.
- Ensure each year's budget supports that year's planned activities.

## Common Budget Mistakes

1. **Budget-narrative mismatch**: Funding for work not described, or work described without funding.
2. **Effort inconsistency**: Effort that does not match the scope, or over-commitment across awards.
3. **Misclassified costs**: Equipment vs. supplies, or direct costs that belong in indirect.
4. **Ignoring the indirect-cost base**: Forgetting which categories are excluded from F&A.
5. **Unjustified travel or consultants**: Line items with no rationale.
6. **Exceeding caps or ceilings**: Salary cap, modular threshold, or program-specific limits.
7. **Weak subaward documentation**: Missing collaborator budgets or scopes.

## Checklist Before Submission

- [ ] Every line item maps to an activity in the narrative.
- [ ] Personnel effort is realistic and within total-effort limits.
- [ ] Fringe and indirect rates match your institution's negotiated agreement.
- [ ] Salary cap, modular threshold, and program ceilings are respected.
- [ ] Equipment vs. supplies classification is correct.
- [ ] Travel and consultant costs are specifically justified.
- [ ] Subaward budgets and scopes are attached and referenced.
- [ ] Multi-year escalation and phasing align with the timeline.
- [ ] Your sponsored-programs office has reviewed the figures.
