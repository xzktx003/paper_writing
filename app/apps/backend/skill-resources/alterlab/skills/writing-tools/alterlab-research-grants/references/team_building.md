# Team Building and Demonstrating Qualifications

## Overview

Reviewers fund teams as much as ideas. A strong proposal makes the case that *this* team — with *this* combination of expertise, track record, and resources — is uniquely positioned to deliver the proposed work. The "Investigator(s)" and "Environment" criteria carry real weight, and a mismatched or under-qualified team can sink an otherwise compelling project.

**Core Principle**: Team composition should be *necessary*, not merely convenient. Every member should fill a gap that the project genuinely needs.

## Demonstrating Team Qualifications

Effective team presentations cover:
- **PI qualifications**: Relevant expertise, leadership capacity, and track record in the research area.
- **Co-investigator and collaborator roles**: Specific, complementary contributions — not a roster of prestigious names.
- **Complementary expertise**: How the members' skills combine to cover every requirement of the work.
- **Prior collaboration**: Evidence the team has worked together productively (shared publications, prior projects).
- **Institutional support and resources**: Facilities, instrumentation, and environment that increase the probability of success.
- **Mentoring and training plans**: For students and postdocs, where relevant.

## Defining Roles Clearly

Assign each member a concrete role tied to the aims:
- **PI / Lead**: Overall scientific direction, integration, and accountability.
- **Co-Investigators**: Lead specific aims or methodological domains.
- **Collaborators / Consultants**: Provide specialized expertise, access, or services for defined tasks.
- **Postdocs / Students / Staff**: Execute the day-to-day research under supervision.

A useful test: for each person, a reviewer should be able to answer "what would break if this person were removed?" If the answer is "nothing," reconsider their inclusion.

## Biosketches and CVs

Biosketches translate qualifications into the agency's required format. Conventions differ across NSF, NIH, DOE, and DARPA, so confirm the current template for your target agency.

**General principles:**
- Highlight the **most relevant** publications and accomplishments, not the longest possible list.
- Include synergistic activities, leadership roles, and collaborations that demonstrate fit.
- Show trajectory and productivity appropriate to career stage.
- Address career gaps or interruptions factually where the format allows.
- For new and early-career investigators, emphasize potential, training, and the strength of the mentoring/environment rather than apologizing for a shorter record.

> ⚠️ Biosketch formats and page/section requirements change. Use the current agency-provided template and follow it exactly — non-compliant biosketches can trigger administrative return.

## Letters of Collaboration and Support

Letters substantiate the claims made in the narrative:
- **Letters of collaboration** confirm specific commitments — what the collaborator will provide (expertise, access, samples, data, services) and when.
- **Letters of support** express institutional or stakeholder backing.
- Strong letters are **specific** (concrete contributions), signed, and on letterhead. Generic "I support this excellent project" letters add little.
- Resource-sharing or access agreements should match what the proposal assumes (e.g., access to a cohort, instrument, or dataset).

Ensure every collaboration described in the narrative is backed by a corresponding letter, and that no letter promises something the budget or plan contradicts.

## Multi-Institutional and Interdisciplinary Teams

Larger teams add capability but also coordination risk. Strengthen the case by:
- Showing a clear **management and communication plan** (see `timeline_planning.md`).
- Justifying why the problem *requires* multiple institutions or disciplines.
- Demonstrating prior successful collaboration among the partners where possible.
- Defining decision authority and conflict-resolution procedures.
- Aligning subaward budgets and scopes with each partner's role.

NSF requires collaboration/management plans for certain collaborative mechanisms; many agencies expect a coordination plan for complex teams.

## Mentoring and Training Plans

Where students or postdocs are supported (and especially for training-oriented or early-career mechanisms):
- Describe the mentoring structure, expectations, and meeting cadence.
- Address professional development, responsible conduct of research, and career preparation.
- For NSF, connect training to broader impacts; for fellowship/career mechanisms, the mentoring plan is a central, scored element.

## Common Team Mistakes

1. **Name-dropping without roles**: Prestigious co-Is with no defined contribution.
2. **Expertise gaps**: A required skill with no one on the team to cover it.
3. **Over-stuffed teams**: Members who add cost and coordination burden without filling a need.
4. **Generic letters**: Support letters that promise nothing specific.
5. **Narrative–letter mismatch**: Claimed collaborations not backed by letters, or letters promising more than the plan supports.
6. **Ignoring management**: A complex multi-institution team with no coordination plan.
7. **Non-compliant biosketches**: Wrong format, which can cause administrative rejection.

## Checklist Before Finalizing

- [ ] Every team member has a defined, necessary role tied to the aims.
- [ ] The combined expertise covers all requirements of the work.
- [ ] Biosketches follow the current agency format exactly.
- [ ] Each described collaboration has a specific, signed letter.
- [ ] Letters match what the plan and budget assume.
- [ ] A management/coordination plan exists for multi-institution teams.
- [ ] Mentoring/training plans are included where students or postdocs are supported.
- [ ] Institutional resources and environment are documented.

For agency-specific biosketch formats and team expectations, see `nsf_guidelines.md`, `nih_guidelines.md`, `doe_guidelines.md`, `darpa_guidelines.md`, and `nstc_guidelines.md`.
