# Resubmission Strategies: Responding to Reviews

> ⚠️ **Verify current policy**: Resubmission rules — the number of allowed resubmissions, page limits for the introduction, and submission windows — change over time and differ by agency and mechanism. Confirm the current policy for your specific opportunity before acting on the conventions described here.

## Overview

Most funded proposals are not funded on the first try. Rejection is a normal, recoverable stage of the process. A strong resubmission treats the prior reviews as data: it diagnoses *why* the proposal fell short and addresses those reasons substantively. Reviewers of a resubmission want to see that you listened, that you improved the science, and that you did so without being defensive.

**Core Principle**: Respond to the *substance* of the critique, not just its surface. Cosmetic changes to a fundamentally questioned proposal rarely succeed.

## Reading Reviews Productively

- **Wait before reacting.** Reviews can sting; let the emotional response pass before you analyze them.
- **Separate signal from noise.** Distinguish criticisms that reflect genuine weaknesses from those that reflect a reviewer misreading the proposal — both require a response, but different kinds.
- **Look for patterns.** Concerns raised by multiple reviewers are your highest priority.
- **Read the summary statement / panel summary carefully.** It often weights which concerns drove the score.
- **Consult the program officer.** Program staff can clarify whether the concerns are fixable and whether resubmission is advisable.

## Diagnosing the Real Problem

Map each critique to a category, because the right response depends on the cause:
- **Misunderstanding**: The reviewer missed or misread something → clarify and strengthen the writing.
- **Genuine weakness**: A real gap in preliminary data, methods, or significance → fix the science.
- **Scope/feasibility**: Too ambitious for the timeline/budget → narrow or restructure aims.
- **Fit**: Wrong program, mechanism, or framing → reposition or consider a different opportunity.

## NIH Resubmission

NIH allows resubmission of an application (historically designated "A1") subject to current policy on the number of resubmissions and timing.

**Introduction to Resubmission** (page limit set by current policy — confirm it):
- Summarize the major criticisms from the prior review.
- Describe the specific changes made in response, point by point.
- Use a clear, organized format (e.g., grouped responses) so reviewers can follow.
- Be respectful and non-defensive about reviewers' comments.
- Highlight the most substantial improvements.

**Strategies:**
- Address **every major criticism**; do not ignore concerns hoping they were minor.
- Make changes substantive — new preliminary data, revised aims, strengthened methods.
- Avoid mechanically using track changes in the final document unless the format calls for marked changes; instead, summarize changes in the introduction.
- Consider restructuring or replacing an aim that reviewers found fundamentally flawed.
- Seek external feedback before resubmitting.

## NSF Resubmission

NSF permits resubmission after revision, with some differences from NIH:
- There is typically no formal, separate "introduction to resubmission" section; instead, the revised proposal itself must address prior concerns (some programs request a response — check the solicitation).
- The proposal may be reviewed by the same or a different panel, so do not assume continuity of reviewers.
- Program-officer feedback is valuable in deciding how and whether to revise.
- Resubmission usually waits for the next submission cycle.

## DOE, DARPA, and NSTC

- **DOE**: Practices vary by office and FOA; some encourage revised submissions to subsequent calls. Use any debrief offered.
- **DARPA**: The model is program-manager-centric and solicitation-driven; a "resubmission" often means responding to a new BAA or an invited full proposal after an abstract/white paper stage. Engage the program manager where the process permits.
- **NSTC**: Revised proposals are typically submitted to a later cycle; address the prior review comments in the new submission.

In all cases, confirm the specific opportunity's rules rather than assuming the NIH/NSF model transfers.

## Strengthening the Resubmission

- **Add preliminary data** that directly answers feasibility concerns.
- **Tighten the significance and innovation** framing if reviewers found them unclear.
- **Improve clarity and organization** so misreadings are less likely.
- **Re-scope** overambitious plans into a focused, achievable set of aims.
- **Refresh the literature and positioning** if the field has moved.

## When Not to Resubmit (As Is)

Reconsider a straightforward resubmission when reviews reveal:
- **Fundamental conceptual flaws** in the hypothesis or framework.
- **Insufficient innovation or significance** that no revision can manufacture.
- **Missing essential expertise or resources** on the team.
- **Need for extensive new work** that effectively makes it a different project — in which case a fresh submission (where allowed) may be more appropriate than a constrained resubmission.

## Common Resubmission Mistakes

1. **Defensiveness**: Arguing with reviewers instead of addressing concerns.
2. **Cosmetic changes**: Minor edits to a proposal with deeper problems.
3. **Selective response**: Addressing easy critiques and ignoring the hard ones.
4. **Ignoring patterns**: Missing the concern that multiple reviewers shared.
5. **No new evidence**: Re-asserting feasibility without new preliminary data.
6. **Overlooking policy**: Violating current resubmission limits or page rules.

## Checklist Before Resubmitting

- [ ] Every major criticism is identified and categorized.
- [ ] Each concern has a specific, substantive response.
- [ ] New preliminary data or revisions address feasibility critiques.
- [ ] Significance and innovation are clearer than before.
- [ ] The introduction (where required) is respectful and well-organized.
- [ ] Current agency policy on resubmission limits, page lengths, and timing is confirmed.
- [ ] The program officer was consulted where helpful.
- [ ] External colleagues reviewed the revised proposal.
