# Qualitative Research Frameworks Reference

## 1. Braun & Clarke's Reflexive Thematic Analysis (2006, 2019, 2021)

### Evolution of Thematic Analysis

Braun & Clarke distinguish three schools of thematic analysis:

| School | Approach | Coding | Theme Development |
|--------|----------|--------|-------------------|
| Coding reliability | Codebook developed early; multiple coders for reliability (Cohen's kappa) | Structured, consensus-based | Themes as domain summaries |
| Codebook | Codebook developed iteratively; structured process | Template-guided | Themes as structured categories |
| Reflexive (Braun & Clarke) | No codebook at start; organic coding process | Researcher-driven, iterative | Themes as patterns of shared meaning |

### 15 Common Mistakes in Thematic Analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2013)

1. Failing to actually analyze the data (just paraphrasing)
2. Using data collection questions as themes
3. Weak or unconvincing analysis with insufficient evidence
4. Mismatch between data and claims
5. Mismatch between theory and method
6. Not providing sufficient context in the write-up
7. Failing to distinguish between a topic and a theme
8. Failing to provide a coherent narrative about the data
9. Analysis that does not address the research question
10. Treating themes as discrete entities rather than interconnected
11. Presenting themes without interpretation
12. Analyzing at only one level (semantic OR latent, not both)
13. Not attending to negative/disconfirming cases
14. Not being reflexive about the analytical process
15. Not distinguishing between thematic analysis as a method and a methodology

### Semantic vs. Latent Coding

```
DATA EXTRACT: "I always stay late because everyone else does.
               If I leave at 5, people look at me funny."

SEMANTIC CODES (what is explicitly said):
- Working late
- Peer pressure about hours
- Social judgment for leaving on time

LATENT CODES (underlying meanings, assumptions, ideologies):
- Presenteeism culture
- Conformity and surveillance
- Equating presence with productivity
- Gendered time norms (if analyzed in context)
```

## 2. Grounded Theory Variants

### Three Main Traditions

| Feature | Classic (Glaser, 1978) | Systematic (Strauss & Corbin, 1998) | Constructivist (Charmaz, 2014) |
|---------|----------------------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Ontology | Realist — theory is discovered | Post-positivist — theory emerges through systematic procedures | Constructivist — theory is co-constructed |
| Literature review | Delay until categories emerge | Integrate throughout | Use as sensitizing concepts |
| Coding stages | Open → selective → theoretical | Open → axial → selective | Initial → focused → theoretical |
| Use of paradigm model | No | Yes (conditions → phenomenon → strategies → consequences) | No (too prescriptive) |
| Memo writing | Conceptual, theoretical | Procedural and analytical | Reflexive, situational |
| Saturation | Theoretical saturation of categories | Theoretical saturation | Theoretical sufficiency |
| Researcher role | Neutral observer | Systematic analyst | Active co-constructor |

### Strauss & Corbin Paradigm Model in Detail

```
CAUSAL CONDITIONS
(What events or incidents led to the phenomenon?)
        │
        ▼
    PHENOMENON
    (The central idea, event, or happening)
        │
        ├──→ CONTEXT
        │    (Specific conditions that influence strategies)
        │
        ├──→ INTERVENING CONDITIONS
        │    (Broader conditions that facilitate or constrain strategies)
        │
        ├──→ ACTION/INTERACTION STRATEGIES
        │    (How participants handle, manage, or respond to the phenomenon)
        │
        └──→ CONSEQUENCES
             (Outcomes of action/interaction strategies)
```

### Theoretical Sampling Procedure

```markdown
## Theoretical Sampling Log

| Round | Category Under Development | Sampling Decision | Data Collected | Outcome |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|----------------|---------|
| 1 | Initial purposive sample | 8 early-career faculty across 4 departments | 8 interviews | Identified core categories: isolation, mentorship gaps, identity negotiation |
| 2 | Mentorship gaps | Recruit faculty who HAD strong mentors (negative case) | 4 interviews | Refined: mentorship quality matters more than presence |
| 3 | Identity negotiation | Recruit faculty who changed disciplines | 3 interviews | New dimension: disciplinary border-crossing adds identity complexity |
| 4 | Saturation check | Re-interview 2 participants from Round 1 | 2 follow-up interviews | No new properties — saturation achieved for core categories |
```

## 3. Phenomenological Approaches Comparison

| Feature | Descriptive (Husserl/Moustakas) | Hermeneutic (Heidegger/van Manen) | IPA (Smith) |
|---------|-------------------------------|-----------------------------------|-------------|
| Goal | Describe the essence of experience | Interpret the meaning of experience | Understand individuals' meaning-making |
| Bracketing | Required — suspend presuppositions (epoché) | Not fully possible — fore-understanding is acknowledged | Attempted but acknowledged as incomplete |
| Analysis method | Horizontalization → textural description → structural description → essence | Thematic reflection, writing and rewriting | Line-by-line → emergent themes → superordinate themes |
| Sample | 5-25 who have experienced the phenomenon | Varies; experiential accounts | 3-10 homogeneous participants |
| Output | Composite description of the essence | Rich interpretive account | Detailed idiographic then cross-case analysis |
| Key text | Moustakas (1994) | van Manen (2016) | Smith, Flowers & Larkin (2009) |

### Moustakas' Modified van Kaam Method

1. Listing and preliminary grouping (horizontalization)
2. Reduction and elimination (test each expression: does it contain a moment of the experience that is necessary and sufficient?)
3. Clustering and thematizing invariant constituents
4. Final identification of invariant constituents and themes (validation)
5. Individual textural description (what was experienced)
6. Individual structural description (how it was experienced)
7. Composite textural-structural description (the essence)

## 4. Ethnographic Methods

### Key Concepts

- **Thick description** (Geertz, 1973): Not just recording behavior but interpreting the layers of meaning in cultural actions
- **Emic vs. Etic**: Insider (participant) perspective vs. outsider (researcher) perspective
- **Cultural immersion**: Extended engagement (typically months to years) in the research setting
- **Participant observation**: Spectrum from complete observer to complete participant

### Field Notes Structure

```markdown
## Field Notes — [Date] [Time] [Location]

### Descriptive Notes (what happened)
[Detailed, chronological account of events, dialogue, setting, participants, actions]

### Reflective Notes (researcher's thoughts)
[Hunches, impressions, feelings, preliminary interpretations, questions for follow-up]

### Methodological Notes (research process)
[Decisions made, problems encountered, changes to protocol, sampling decisions]

### Theoretical Notes (connections to literature/theory)
[Links to concepts, theories, prior findings; emerging analytical ideas]
```

## 5. Narrative Inquiry Framework (Clandinin & Connelly, 2000)

### Three-Dimensional Narrative Inquiry Space

| Dimension | Description | Analytical Focus |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Temporality | Past, present, future | How does the story unfold over time? What is the narrative arc? |
| Sociality | Personal (feelings, hopes, dispositions) and social (environment, people, culture) | What is the interplay between inner experience and social context? |
| Place | Physical and relational spaces | How does place shape the experience and the story told? |

### Restorying Process

1. Collect stories (interviews, journals, artifacts)
2. Retell stories in chronological sequence
3. Identify narrative elements: characters, setting, problem, actions, resolution
4. Code for themes within and across narratives
5. Compose research texts that honor participants' stories while offering scholarly interpretation

## 6. Quality Criteria by Methodology

| Methodology | Primary Quality Framework | Key Criteria |
|-------------|--------------------------|--------------|
| Thematic Analysis | Braun & Clarke's 15-point checklist; trustworthiness | Systematic coding, researcher reflexivity, coherent themes |
| Grounded Theory | Glaser: fit, workability, relevance, modifiability; Corbin & Strauss: credibility, resonance, usefulness | Theoretical sampling, saturation, audit trail |
| IPA | Yardley (2000): sensitivity to context, commitment and rigor, transparency and coherence, impact and importance | Idiographic focus, verbatim evidence, interpretive depth |
| Ethnography | Thick description, prolonged engagement, triangulation | Cultural authenticity, member reflections, reflexivity |
| Case Study | Yin: construct validity, internal validity, external validity, reliability | Chain of evidence, pattern matching, cross-case synthesis |
| Narrative Inquiry | Verisimilitude, resonance, transferability | Narrative coherence, participant voice, relational ethics |
