# Research Ethics Guidelines Reference

## 1. The Belmont Report (1979) — Extended Summary

### Historical Context

The Belmont Report was issued by the National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects of Biomedical and Behavioral Research in response to the Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932-1972), where African American men with syphilis were deliberately left untreated to study disease progression, even after effective treatment (penicillin) became available in the 1940s.

### Distinguishing Practice from Research

| Feature | Practice | Research |
|---------|----------|----------|
| Purpose | Enhance well-being of individual patient/client | Contribute to generalizable knowledge |
| Design | Tailored to individual needs | Follows a protocol for systematic investigation |
| Outcome | Expected benefit to individual | May or may not benefit participants |
| Ethical oversight | Professional standards, licensing | IRB review, informed consent, regulatory compliance |

Note: Quality improvement activities may blur this boundary. Consult your IRB when activities serve both purposes.

### Three Principles Applied

**Respect for Persons → Informed Consent**

Three elements:
1. **Information**: Disclose research procedure, purposes, risks, benefits, alternatives, and opportunity to ask questions
2. **Comprehension**: Present information in a manner adapted to the participant's capacity; consider language, literacy, cognitive ability
3. **Voluntariness**: Free from coercion (threat of harm) and undue influence (excessive reward or authority pressure)

**Beneficence → Risk-Benefit Assessment**

Systematic assessment must consider:
- Nature and probability of harms (physical, psychological, social, economic, legal)
- Nature and probability of benefits (to participant, to others, to society)
- Alternative approaches that might achieve the same knowledge with less risk
- Whether the research design is sound enough to yield valid results (poorly designed research exposes participants to risk without benefit)

**Justice → Fair Selection of Participants**

- Do not select participants simply because they are available, convenient, or easily manipulated
- Do not systematically exclude groups from research benefits
- Consider historical patterns of exploitation (e.g., selecting prisoners, institutionalized persons, or minority groups for risky research while directing benefits to privileged populations)
- Ensure fair distribution of research burdens and benefits across social groups

## 2. Common Rule (45 CFR 46) — Key Provisions

### Categories of Review

| Category | Criteria | Examples |
|----------|----------|---------|
| **Exempt** | Minimal risk; fits specific categories (educational research, surveys/interviews with non-sensitive topics, public observation, secondary data analysis) | Anonymous survey of adults on non-sensitive topics; analysis of de-identified publicly available data |
| **Expedited** | Minimal risk; fits specific categories but requires consent documentation | Survey with identifiers; interviews on moderately sensitive topics; blood draws from healthy adults |
| **Full Board** | More than minimal risk; involves vulnerable populations; sensitive topics | Drug trials; deception studies; research with prisoners; invasive procedures |

### Informed Consent Elements (45 CFR 46.116)

**Required Elements:**
1. Statement that study involves research; explanation of purposes, duration, and procedures
2. Description of reasonably foreseeable risks or discomforts
3. Description of benefits to participant or others
4. Disclosure of appropriate alternative procedures or treatments
5. Description of confidentiality protections
6. For more-than-minimal-risk research: explanation of compensation and available treatments for injury
7. Contact information for questions about research, rights, and injuries
8. Statement that participation is voluntary; refusal or withdrawal without penalty

**Additional Elements (when appropriate):**
9. Statement that the treatment/procedure may involve unforeseeable risks
10. Circumstances under which participation may be terminated by investigator
11. Additional costs to participant
12. Consequences of withdrawal and procedures for orderly termination
13. Statement that significant new findings will be provided to participant
14. Approximate number of participants

### 2018 Common Rule Revisions (Key Changes)

- Expanded exemption categories
- New requirement for single-IRB review for multi-site studies
- Consent forms must begin with concise summary of key information
- Broad consent option for secondary research use of identifiable biospecimens/data
- Cooperative research must rely on a single IRB (with exceptions)

## 3. Declaration of Helsinki — Key Provisions (2024 revision)

The current version was adopted at the WMA's 75th General Assembly in October 2024 and is the only official version (earlier revisions are for historical reference only). It replaces "subjects" with "participants" throughout, extends duties to all individuals and organizations involved in research (not only physicians), and strengthens scientific-integrity and community-engagement provisions. The 2024 revision renumbered paragraphs relative to the 2013 text, so confirm any paragraph number against the current document before citing it; the provisions below are stated by topic.

| Topic | Provision |
|-------|-----------|
| Purpose of medical research | To understand the causes, development, and effects of diseases and to improve interventions |
| Scientific soundness | Research must conform to generally accepted scientific principles and be based on adequate knowledge of the scientific literature |
| Vulnerability | Some groups and individuals are particularly vulnerable and need specifically considered protection |
| Qualifications | Research must be conducted by appropriately qualified and trained persons |
| Compensation for harm | Participants who suffer harm are entitled to appropriate compensation and treatment |
| Inclusion of vulnerable groups | Justified only when the research responds to their health needs/priorities and cannot be carried out in a non-vulnerable group |
| Ethics committee review | The protocol must be submitted for consideration, comment, guidance, and approval to a research ethics committee before the study begins |
| Informed consent | Freely given informed consent required, preferably in writing |
| Use of placebo | Acceptable when no proven intervention exists, or when compelling methodological reasons justify it and participants will not be subject to serious or irreversible harm |
| Trial registration | Every study involving human participants must be registered in a publicly accessible database before recruitment of the first participant |
| Publication of results | Researchers have a duty to make results publicly available, including negative and inconclusive results |

## 4. GDPR for Research — Detailed Guidance

### Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Personal data | Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person |
| Processing | Any operation performed on personal data (collection, recording, storage, analysis, sharing, destruction) |
| Controller | Entity that determines the purposes and means of processing |
| Processor | Entity that processes data on behalf of the controller |
| Special categories of data | Racial/ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data, health data, sex life/orientation |

### Article 89 — Safeguards for Research Processing

Research may benefit from derogations from certain data subject rights IF appropriate safeguards are implemented:
- Technical and organizational measures (pseudonymization, encryption)
- Data minimization
- Purpose limitation

Member States may provide further conditions, including:
- Restrictions on rights of access, rectification, restriction, and objection
- These derogations apply only insofar as rights would render impossible or seriously impair the research

### Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) — When Required

A DPIA is mandatory when processing is "likely to result in a high risk":
- Systematic and extensive profiling with significant effects
- Large-scale processing of special categories of data
- Systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas
- Processing involving vulnerable individuals (patients, children, employees)
- Innovative use of technology with personal data
- Processing that prevents individuals from exercising a right or using a service

### Cross-Border Data Transfers

| Mechanism | When to Use |
|-----------|-------------|
| Adequacy decision | Transfer to country with adequate data protection (EU Commission list) |
| Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) | Transfer to country without adequacy decision |
| Binding Corporate Rules | Intra-group transfers within multinational organizations |
| Explicit consent | Participant informed of risks and specifically consents to transfer |
| Research derogation (Art. 49(1)(d)) | Transfer necessary for important reasons of public interest |

## 5. Research Integrity Frameworks

### CRediT (Contributor Roles Taxonomy)

| Role | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Conceptualization | Ideas; formulation or evolution of overarching research goals and aims |
| Methodology | Development or design of methodology; creation of models |
| Software | Programming, software development; designing computer programs; implementation of code |
| Validation | Verification of overall replication/reproducibility of results |
| Formal analysis | Application of statistical, mathematical, computational, or other formal techniques |
| Investigation | Conducting research and investigation process, specifically data/evidence collection |
| Resources | Provision of study materials, reagents, materials, patients, laboratory samples, instrumentation, computing resources |
| Data curation | Management activities to annotate, scrub, and maintain research data |
| Writing — original draft | Preparation and creation of the published work, specifically writing the initial draft |
| Writing — review & editing | Critical review, commentary, or revision of the published work |
| Visualization | Preparation, creation, and/or presentation of the published work, specifically visualization/data presentation |
| Supervision | Oversight and leadership responsibility for the research activity planning and execution |
| Project administration | Management and coordination responsibility for the research activity planning and execution |
| Funding acquisition | Acquisition of the financial support for the project |

### ICMJE Authorship Criteria

All four criteria must be met:
1. Substantial contributions to conception OR design OR acquisition OR analysis OR interpretation of data
2. Drafting the article OR revising it critically for important intellectual content
3. Final approval of the version to be published
4. Agreement to be accountable for all aspects of the work

### Reporting Guidelines

| Study Type | Guideline | Purpose |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Randomized trials | CONSORT | Complete and transparent reporting of RCTs |
| Observational studies | STROBE | Reporting standards for cohort, case-control, cross-sectional studies |
| Systematic reviews | PRISMA | Transparent reporting of systematic reviews and meta-analyses |
| Diagnostic accuracy | STARD | Complete reporting of diagnostic accuracy studies |
| Qualitative research | COREQ | Consolidated criteria for reporting qualitative research |
| Case reports | CARE | Reporting of case reports |
| Animal research | ARRIVE | Reporting of in vivo experiments |
| Quality improvement | SQUIRE | Standards for quality improvement reporting excellence |

## 6. Animal Research Ethics

### The 3Rs (Russell & Burch, 1959)

| Principle | Definition | Implementation |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Replacement** | Methods that avoid or replace the use of animals | In vitro methods, computer models, human volunteers, lower organisms |
| **Reduction** | Methods that minimize the number of animals used | Appropriate statistical design, sharing data/tissues, improved experimental design |
| **Refinement** | Methods that minimize suffering and improve welfare | Better housing, anesthesia, analgesia, humane endpoints, training |

### IACUC Review Requirements

- Species justification (why this animal model?)
- Number justification (power analysis or other statistical rationale)
- Pain and distress categorization (USDA pain categories)
- Veterinary care plan
- Humane endpoints criteria
- Personnel qualifications and training
- Housing and husbandry standards
